Important: How your task differs from the previous experiment

What you just read vs. what you will do

The instructions you just read described how participants in the previous experiment played. In that session, Player B directly guessed the secret number by choosing a number between 1 and 7.

Your task is different. Instead of entering a direct guess, you will report your belief about how often Player A lied — that is, how often Player A sent a message that was not the true secret number (i.e., did not delegate to the computer). You will move a slider from 0% (Player A always told the truth) to 100% (Player A always lied).

How the computer uses your belief

Once you report your belief, the computer will automatically calculate and submit the guess that best maximises your chances of earning the bonus given what you stated.

This means your best strategy is to report your true belief — the more accurately your belief reflects Player A's actual behaviour, the higher your expected earnings.

The next pages will ask some comprehension questions and then show you a short tutorial of the belief slider before the game begins.

How the computer calculates the optimal guess

Let p be your stated belief (as a fraction between 0 and 1) about how often Player A lied, and let m be the message you observe. The computer submits:

Optimal guess = (1 − p) × m + p × 4

This formula follows maximizes your expected earnings. When Player A tells the truth (probability 1 − p), the message m equals the secret number, so the best guess is m. When Player A lies (probability p), the message carries no information and the best guess is the unconditional mean of the secret number, which is 4. The optimal guess is a weighted average of these two cases.
