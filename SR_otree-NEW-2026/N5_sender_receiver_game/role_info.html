How your task differs from the previous experiment

The instructions you just read described how participants in the previous experiment played. In that experiment, Player B had a 20 second window to guess the secret number by choosing a number between 1 and 7, without knowing whether Player A delegated to the computer.

Your task differs from the previous experiment in three ways:

  1. The messages you see today are recycled from Player A's in the previous experiment.
  2. You will have a 60 second window to submit your response.
  3. Instead of entering a direct guess, you will report your belief about the likelihood that Player A delegated to the computer using a slider from 0% (Player A surely did not delegate) to 100% (Player A surely delegated). The computer then automatically calculates and submits the optimal guess for you.
How the computer uses your belief

Once you report your belief, the computer will automatically calculate and submit the guess that best maximises your chances of earning the bonus given what you stated.

This means your best strategy is to report your true belief — the more accurately your belief reflects Player A's actual behaviour, the higher your expected earnings.

How the computer calculates the optimal guess

Let p be your stated belief (as a fraction between 0 and 1) about the likelihood that Player A delegated to the computer (was honest), and let m be the message you observe. The computer submits:

Optimal guess = p × m + (1 − p) × 4

This formula maximizes your expected earnings. When Player A is honest (probability p), the message m equals the secret number, so the best guess is m. When Player A is not honest (probability 1 − p), the message carries no information and the best guess is the unconditional mean of the secret number, which is 4. The optimal guess is a weighted average of these two cases.
